Health Alert

Tick-Borne Illness

Ticks carrying Lyme disease and other illnesses are present in the Lake Julia area. Check yourself, children, and pets after time outdoors — especially in wooded and grassy areas.

Deer ticks (black-legged ticks) are the primary concern and the most common carrier of Lyme disease. Symptoms include a bullseye rash, fever, fatigue, and joint pain. Early treatment is effective — consult a doctor promptly if you suspect exposure.

Lone star ticks, while less prevalent in Wisconsin, have been documented in the state. Their bite can transmit Alpha-gal syndrome — an allergic reaction to red meat that can develop weeks after a bite and persist for years.

American dog ticks are also present and can carry Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever, a serious bacterial infection causing fever, headache, and a spotted rash. Though less common, it can be severe if not treated promptly.

For more information, visit the CDC Lyme Disease page or see the Wisconsin DHS tick identification guide.

Wildlife

Beavers & Otters

Beavers are active in the Lake Julia watershed. While they are a natural part of the ecosystem, beaver activity can affect shoreline vegetation, water levels, and drainage structures. If beaver activity is affecting your property, contact Oneida County for guidance on management options.

North American River Otters are also present in the watershed. Otters tend to be more active during the day in winter and more nocturnal the rest of the year. They are known for being playful and social, though they can be aggressive toward humans and pets when cornered or protecting their young.

Fishing

Fishing on Lake Julia

Lake Julia supports a healthy fishery. A professional lake study by Onterra LLC (2010–13) confirmed the lake's good overall ecological health. All Wisconsin fishing regulations apply. Licenses and regulations are available through the Wisconsin DNR.

Forest

Forest Ecology

The forests surrounding Lake Julia were significantly shaped by the Civilian Conservation Corps in the 1930s. Today they support a rich mix of northern hardwoods and conifers. For an interesting look at how forests communicate and function as interconnected systems, see "The Social Life of Trees" — NYT Magazine, Dec. 2020.

Shoreline Stewardship

What you can do

Healthy shoreline vegetation — native grasses, shrubs, and trees — filters runoff, prevents erosion, and provides critical habitat. Consider maintaining a natural buffer of native plants along your shoreline rather than manicured lawn to the water's edge.

Avoid using fertilizers or pesticides near the water, and minimize impervious surfaces that direct runoff directly into the lake. The Wisconsin DNR's shoreland zoning program provides guidance on best practices and regulations.

Conservation Organizations

Regional resources

Lake Julia spans both Oneida and Forest Counties. The Forest County Association of Lakes works on water quality and lake stewardship across Forest County.

The Oneida County Land and Water Conservation Department (LWCD) provides resources and programs for shoreline management, erosion control, and water quality protection.
