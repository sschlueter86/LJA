Articles of Incorporation

What is this document?

The legal paperwork filed with the State of Wisconsin in July 2004 that officially created the Lake Julia Association, Inc. as a recognized nonprofit corporation.

What does it say, in plain English?

  • We're official. The Lake Julia Association is a legally incorporated nonprofit under Wisconsin law, filed and stamped by the Wisconsin Department of Financial Institutions.
  • Our mission. We exist to protect, preserve, and improve the properties, water quality, wildlife, and general welfare of Lake Julia and its surrounding areas in Oneida and Forest Counties.
  • We're a nonprofit. No profits are distributed to individuals. We operate like a charity under IRS 501(c)(3) rules, meaning donations may be tax-deductible.
  • No politics. The association cannot campaign for political candidates or spend a substantial portion of its activities trying to influence legislation.
  • We have members. Membership rules are defined in the Bylaws.
  • We have a Board. A Board of Directors (minimum 3, initially 6) manages the association's affairs.
  • If we ever dissolve, any remaining assets go to another qualifying nonprofit — nothing goes to private individuals.
  • Changes require a vote. Amending these Articles requires a 2/3 vote of members or a majority of the Board.

Bylaws

What is this document?

The Bylaws are the association's rulebook — the day-to-day operating procedures that govern how the Lake Julia Association actually runs.

Members

  • Membership is open to property owners and residents on the Lake Julia shoreline, as well as others who support the association's mission.
  • Each paid membership gets one vote.
  • Annual dues are set by the Board at the start of each year. Membership lapses if dues aren't paid by December 31st.
  • The annual membership meeting is held in July or August each year.

Board of Directors

  • The Board consists of 6 directors, each serving 3-year rotating terms (so roughly 2 seats turn over each year).
  • Directors are elected by members at the annual meeting.
  • The Board manages the association's affairs, but any single expense over $750 must be approved by the membership.
  • The Board can act between meetings via written consent if 2/3 of directors agree.

Officers

  • The association has a President, Vice President, Secretary, and Treasurer.
  • Officers are elected annually by the Board.
  • The President runs day-to-day operations. The Secretary keeps records and minutes. The Treasurer manages finances and reports to the Board and members annually.

Finances

  • No loans can be taken out without Board authorization.
  • All checks and financial instruments must be signed by authorized officers.
  • The fiscal year end date is set by the Board.

Other Rules

  • The Bylaws can be amended by a 2/3 vote of members present at a meeting where a quorum exists, provided members were given advance notice of the proposed change.
  • The association is expected to join county and state lake organizations (like WLIA) and appoint delegates.
  • Directors and officers are indemnified (legally protected) when acting in good faith on behalf of the association.

2013 Comprehensive Management Plan

What is this document?

A 75-page professional assessment of Lake Julia's ecological health, commissioned by the Lake Julia Association and funded in part by Wisconsin DNR grants. It was researched and written by Onterra LLC between 2010 and 2013, with input from a community planning committee and a survey of lake residents.

The bottom line

  • Lake Julia is in exceptional shape. When compared to similar lakes across Wisconsin and within the Northern Lakes and Forests region, Lake Julia ranks as having excellent water quality — low nutrient levels, no algae problems, and no invasive aquatic plants at the time of the study.
  • The watershed protects the lake. About 58% of the 5,363-acre watershed surrounding the lake is wetlands, and another 31% is forest. These natural land covers filter runoff before it reaches the lake, keeping nutrient inputs low.
  • The plant community is remarkable. Surveyors found 58 native aquatic plant species — an extraordinarily high number. The lake's Floristic Quality Index score (a measure of ecological health) was nearly double the state and regional averages, indicating that many of the plants present are species that only survive in pristine conditions.
  • No invasive plants were found. Despite nearby lakes battling Eurasian water milfoil (Virgin Lake, Long Lake, Eagle River Chain), Lake Julia was free of this and other invasive species at the time of the survey. The only invasive species present were rusty crayfish.

Water quality findings

  • The lake is classified as mesotrophic — meaning it falls in a healthy middle range between nutrient-poor and nutrient-rich. It has enough productivity to support fish and wildlife, but not so much that algae blooms are a problem.
  • Phosphorus levels ranked Excellent compared to similar Wisconsin lakes. Phosphorus is the nutrient that drives algae growth, so keeping it low is the primary goal of lake management.
  • The water has a natural dark, tea-like color caused by organic tannins that leach from surrounding wetlands and forests — not pollution. This tint limits light penetration, which actually helps keep invasive plant growth in check.
  • The lake is not sensitive to acid rain. Its alkalinity is sufficient to buffer against acidic inputs, and its pH is within normal range for Wisconsin lakes.
  • No evidence of faulty septic systems was found during the study period.

Shoreline condition

  • 70% of Lake Julia's 6.9-mile shoreline is natural and undeveloped — a rarity for Wisconsin lakes, and a major reason the lake is in such good ecological health.
  • Only about 4% (0.2 miles) of the shoreline showed significant human disturbance. Protecting what remains natural was identified as more important than restoring what has already been developed.
  • Natural shorelands filter runoff, prevent erosion, provide wildlife habitat, and protect spawning areas for fish. Clearing vegetation, building seawalls, or mowing to the water's edge degrades all of these functions.

Fishery

  • The lake supports a diverse mix of game fish including walleye, muskellunge, smallmouth bass, northern pike, and panfish. The WDNR actively stocks walleye (on even years) and muskellunge (on odd years) to supplement natural reproduction.
  • Because Lake Julia is relatively nutrient-poor, it will never produce the large numbers of trophy-sized fish found in more productive lakes — this is a natural characteristic, not a management failure.
  • Lake Julia falls within Native American ceded territory under the Treaty of 1842, which allows tribal members a regulated spear harvest. In practice, spearing has been rare — the only recorded harvest was 25 walleye in 2003.
  • Mercury has accumulated in larger walleye. The WDNR recommends that walleye over 15 inches be eaten no more than once a month by adults, and not at all by women of childbearing age or children under 15.

What the plan recommended

  • Communicate and educate. Form an Education Committee to distribute newsletters, promote responsible boating, shoreland stewardship, and awareness of lake ecology among residents.
  • Monitor water quality. Enroll volunteers in the WDNR's Citizens Lake Monitoring Network to collect ongoing Secchi disk (water clarity) data, building a long-term record for the lake.
  • Prevent invasive species. Continue and expand the Clean Boats Clean Waters (CBCW) boat inspection program at the public landing, and conduct annual volunteer "lake sweeps" to detect any early invasive plant arrivals before they can establish.
  • Work with fisheries managers. Maintain contact with WDNR fisheries staff, explore habitat enhancement options (such as fish cribs or brush structures), and share fishing information with the community.
